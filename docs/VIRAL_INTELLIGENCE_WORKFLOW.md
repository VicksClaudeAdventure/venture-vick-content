# Viral Intelligence Workflow

## Purpose

This document is the research and analysis methodology used during Steps 1-5 of the Content Pipeline Playbook. It defines how to deconstruct breakout videos, map psychological triggers, identify narrative structures, and mine patterns. The playbook tells you what to do — this document tells you how to think while doing it.

---

## Jay's Niche

Building life and business in public: starting a business, self-improvement lessons, financial education, real estate investment, first rental property renovation (Philly), journey to 100k followers.

## Jay's Audience

People with the same struggles who want to achieve the things Jay is currently working through. The viewer persona (Marcus, 24) is defined in the Content Pipeline Playbook.

---

## Content Style Filters

**Include:** Educational, insight-driven, explainer, storytelling with lesson, industry commentary, case studies, breakdowns, authority content

**Reject:** Dance trends, memes, lip syncing, pure entertainment, reaction-only

## 7-Component Deconstruction Framework

Use this to analyze every breakout video in Step 4A. Fill out all 7 components per video.

| # | Component | What to Look For | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Hook** | First sentence / first 3 seconds | What is the exact phrasing? What information gap does it create? Does it lead with the punchline? |
| 2 | **Problem / Stakes** | How fast do they name the pain? | How many seconds in? Is the pain specific or vague? Does Marcus feel this pain? |
| 3 | **Credibility Signal** | How do they establish authority? | Direct claim ("I own 5 properties")? Implied authority (speaks like an insider)? Social proof ("My 100k followers know...")? |
| 4 | **Core Content** | The main teaching or story | Is it one idea or multiple? How is it structured? Problem-solution? Before/after? Steps? |
| 5 | **Proof / Evidence** | Numbers, receipts, examples | Are numbers specific? Are examples relatable? Does it feel real or made up? |
| 6 | **Psychological Trigger** | Which emotion is being activated? | See the 6 Triggers table below. Which is primary? Which is secondary? |
| 7 | **CTA** | Closing ask | Exact words? Action type (follow, comment, save, share)? Does it feel earned or tacked on? |

## 6 Psychological Triggers

Map every breakout video to one primary trigger in Step 4C.

| # | Trigger | What It Does | Example Hook | Jay's Use Case |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **FOMO** | Creates urgency — "others are benefiting, you're not" | "Your neighbors are doing this and you're losing money" | Real estate moves most people don't know about |
| 2 | **Curiosity Gap** | Opens a question the brain must resolve | "The one clause in every lease most landlords miss" | Any "secret" or "most people don't know" angle |
| 3 | **Social Proof** | Validates through others' behavior | "50,000 landlords learned this the hard way" | Case studies, common mistakes, crowd wisdom |
| 4 | **Authority / Insider Knowledge** | Positions creator as the expert insider | "After managing 12 properties, I can tell you..." | Jay's experience-based takes, lessons learned |
| 5 | **Anger / Injustice** | Activates righteous indignation | "Your property manager is keeping this from you" | Exposés, "here's how they're screwing you" content |
| 6 | **Hope / Aspiration** | Makes the desired outcome feel achievable | "You could own your first rental property in 18 months" | Permission slips, roadmaps, achievability content |

**Usage in analysis:**
- Identify which trigger is PRIMARY (the dominant emotional lever)
- Identify which trigger is SECONDARY (supports the primary)
- Track which trigger appears most often across the week's breakouts
- Flag which trigger is underused but high-potential for Jay's audience

## 6 Narrative Structures

Identify the structure of each breakout video in Step 4D.

| # | Structure | Flow | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Problem → Solution** | Name the pain → present the fix → explain why it works | How-to content, tactical advice |
| 2 | **Before → After** | Describe the before state → show the transformation → bridge the gap | Personal stories, results-based content |
| 3 | **Myth → Reality** | State the common belief → bust it → replace with truth | Contrarian takes, authority-building |
| 4 | **Steps / List** | Here are N things → explain each → close with takeaway | Educational content, checklists |
| 5 | **Story → Lesson** | Tell a specific story → extract the universal lesson → apply to viewer | Storytelling, personal brand, relatability |
| 6 | **Question → Answer** | Pose the question → tease the complexity → deliver the clear answer | FAQ-style, debate-format content |

**Usage in analysis:**
- Note which structures are dominating breakouts this week
- Note which structures align with Jay's series formulas (Venture = Story → Lesson, V-Bucks = Problem → Solution or Steps/List, Real Estate = Before → After or Story → Lesson, Authoritative = Myth → Reality or Question → Answer)
- Flag any new structure pattern that doesn't fit the 6 above — it may warrant adding to this list

## Pattern Mining Protocol

Run this after completing individual video analysis (Step 4B). The goal is to find cross-video patterns that inform this week's scripts.

**Only note a pattern if it appears in 3+ videos.** One-offs are noise.

**What to look for:**

**Hook patterns:**
- What hook structures appear in 3+ breakouts?
- What's the average hook word count across breakouts?
- Do breakout hooks tend to start with a number, a question, a statement, or a story?
- Where does the punchline land — word 1, word 5, end of sentence?

**Content patterns:**
- What topics appear in 3+ breakouts?
- How many ideas per video? (Almost always one — but note exceptions)
- What's the ratio of story to teaching in breakout videos?

**Pacing patterns:**
- Where do breakout creators pause? (After hook? Before payoff? After a number?)
- What's the average section length? (Hook → setup → core → payoff → CTA)
- Do breakouts tend to front-load the insight or build to it?

**CTA patterns:**
- What CTA formats appear in 3+ breakouts?
- Do breakout CTAs tend to be specific series pulls, save triggers, comment hooks, or tag-a-friend?
- How long are breakout CTAs? (One sentence? Two?)

**Output:** Pattern summary with specific examples from videos. These patterns feed directly into Step 5 (generating this week's directional insights).

## Format Roster Management

The framework roster (defined in the Content Pipeline Playbook) is a living set. This workflow governs how formats enter and exit the roster.

**Promotion criteria:**
- A format appears in 3+ breakouts in a single week, OR
- A format appears in 2+ breakouts per week for 3 consecutive weeks
- The format is distinct from existing roster entries (not a variation of an existing format)

**Demotion process:**
- If a rostered format doesn't appear in any breakouts for 3 consecutive weeks, flag it
- If it remains absent for 2 more weeks after flagging (5 weeks total), demote it to archive
- Archived formats can be re-promoted if they resurface in breakout data

**Tracking:** In each week's Step 4E analysis, note which rostered formats appeared in breakouts and which didn't. Maintain a running count in the weekly analysis output.

## Viral Probability Scoring

Used in Step 6 to score topic proposals. Each topic gets scored 1-10 across 5 dimensions.

| Dimension | What It Measures | Scoring Guide |
|---|---|---|
| **Curiosity** | Information gap | 1-3: Topic is obvious/expected. 4-6: Mildly interesting. 7-8: Strong "I need to know" pull. 9-10: Impossible to scroll past without knowing. |
| **Shareability** | Send-to-someone potential | 1-3: No clear recipient. 4-6: Generally interesting. 7-8: "I know exactly who to send this to." 9-10: Group chat material. |
| **Authority** | Jay's credibility on this topic | 1-3: Jay has no edge here. 4-6: Jay has some relevant experience. 7-8: Jay has lived this. 9-10: Jay is one of the most credible voices on this. |
| **Originality** | Freshness of the angle | 1-3: Done to death. 4-6: Common topic, slight twist. 7-8: Angle hasn't been covered well. 9-10: Nobody has said this yet. |
| **Emotion** | Feeling strength | 1-3: Neutral. 4-6: Mild interest. 7-8: Clear emotional reaction (fear, hope, anger, pride). 9-10: Visceral — Marcus feels this in his gut. |

**Threshold:** 40+/50 to move forward to Jay's approval gate. Do not inflate scores to meet the threshold.
**Max score:** 50

## Redundancy Rules

When compiling research or merging analysis across videos:

**Eliminate** redundant explanations — don't re-define the same concept in multiple sections.

**Preserve** redundant data points where they add value. Three tests:

1. **Context** — If a hook appears in the analysis AND in a pattern summary, keep it in BOTH. The reader shouldn't have to cross-reference.
2. **Reinforcement** — If a mechanic keeps appearing across multiple videos, that repetition IS the insight. Don't strip it.
3. **Standalone usability** — Each section must work independently. If someone reads only the pattern summary without reading individual analyses, they should still get the full picture.

**Test before removing:** "If someone reads ONLY this section, would they miss something important?" If yes → keep it.
