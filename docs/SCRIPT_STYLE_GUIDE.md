# Script Style Guide

**Jay Victor (@vick) — Vick's Venture**
**Purpose:** Pacing and tone rules for script writing. Read this BEFORE writing any script. These rules override default writing instincts.
**Last Updated:** April 2026

---

## Core Principle

Every sentence must earn its spot. If a sentence restates something the previous sentence already said, delete it. If a sentence previews what the next sentence is about to show, delete it. If a word can be cut without losing meaning, cut it.

---

## Pacing Rules

### 1. Never say the same thing twice in consecutive sentences
This is the #1 script killer. It shows up as:
- Stating something is bad, then listing why it's bad ("It was bare bones. I needed premium features, onboarding, settings, pricing" — the list IS the bare bones, you don't need both)
- Telling the viewer how you felt, then explaining the feeling ("I couldn't stop. By 2 AM I had fourteen done" — the second sentence already proves you couldn't stop)
- Summarizing before showing ("The results were not great. The upload was confusing, the results were a mess..." — the list IS the results not being great)

**Fix:** Delete whichever version is weaker. Keep the one that's more specific or more visual.

### 2. Cut filler words and phrases
These add nothing and slow the pace:
- "just" ("I just dumped all five" → "I dumped all five")
- "sat down" ("I sat down at 9 PM" → dead space, nobody pictures anything)
- "honestly" (always delete)
- "basically" (always delete)
- "so" at the start of sentences (usually cuttable)
- "I told myself I'd just plan it out. Maybe knock out two or three things." → pick one, they're different levels of ambition

### 3. Don't preview what you're about to say
Let the next line be the reveal. If the viewer can predict the next sentence from the current one, the current one is a spoiler, not a setup.

- ❌ "And the wild part is I wasn't tired. It was like starting a video game at 8 PM..." — "the wild part is" tells us something surprising is coming. Just say the surprising thing.
- ✅ "I wasn't even tired. You know when you start a video game at 8 PM and look up and it's 3 AM?"

### 4. Lists should be rhythm devices, not info dumps
When you list things, each item should hit like a beat, building toward something. Don't list for the sake of being comprehensive.

- ❌ "I needed premium features, an onboarding flow, a settings page, pricing — basically everything that makes it feel like a real product." (grocery list + summary of the grocery list)
- ✅ "No onboarding, no pricing, no settings — nothing that makes someone actually pay for it." (rhythm building to the real point)

### 5. If the viewer can see it on screen, don't narrate it
Color palettes, UI screenshots, code on screen — let the visuals work. Describing what's visible is wasted words.

### 6. Let numbers escalate to tell the story
When numbers are doing the work, get out of their way. The progression IS the story.

- ❌ "I told myself I'd just plan it out. Maybe knock out two or three things. But once I started mapping features on the whiteboard I couldn't stop. By 2 AM I had fourteen done. By sunrise all twenty-one were planned and half were already built."
- ✅ "I told myself I'd knock out two, maybe three things. By 2 AM I had fourteen done. By sunrise, twenty-one."

### 7. Get to punchlines faster by cutting setup buffers
"There was one specific change I made that dropped it to eight" → the sentence is about a sentence. Just say the change.

"And when it came together it honestly looked like a company with a legal team made it" → "When I stepped back it looked like a company with a legal team built it."

## Tone Rules

### 1. Every hook must open a curiosity gap
The hook's only job is to make the viewer NEED to know what happens next. If the viewer can guess what the video is about from the hook alone, there's no gap. Announcement hooks ("Here's how much I spent," "Here's what I do," "Let me show you") tell the viewer what's coming — there's nothing left to wonder about.

A curiosity gap means the hook opens a question the viewer cannot answer without watching. "I built an app that was wrong about almost everything. And people were using it." — the viewer has to stay to find out what happened.

- ❌ "Here's exactly how much I spent building my app." (announcement — no question opened)
- ❌ "I sat down at 9 PM to fix one thing and didn't get up until all 21 features were built." (describes what happened — no gap)
- ✅ "I built an app that was wrong about almost everything. And people were using it." (opens a question: what happened?)
- ✅ "If I lost everything tomorrow and had to start over, here's everything I'd do with my money." (hypothetical anyone can project into)

### 2. Cold audience rule — hooks must work for strangers
At under 10K followers, the algorithm tests your video on 300-500 strangers who have never seen your face. If the hook requires context, insider knowledge, or clever wordplay that needs decoding, those strangers will swipe. No jokes that need setup. No references to your app, your brand, or your journey unless the hook works without knowing any of that.

**The test:** Show your hook to someone who has never heard of you. Do they want to keep watching? If they need to know who you are first, the hook fails.

**Case study — what a failed hook looks like:**
Hook: "Here's exactly how much I spent building my app. Every single dollar I spent from idea to live product."
Result: 71.4% skip rate, 4 second average watch time, 1,107 accounts reached.
Why it failed: It's an announcement hook with zero curiosity gap. The text overlay said "MOST COMMON START-UP MISCONCEPTION ($$$)" which is a different promise than the voiceover — two conflicting pitches in 3 seconds. The viewer doesn't know what the app is, doesn't care about the costs, and has no reason to stay. Compare to Jay's typical skip rate goal of under 40%.

### 3. Hooks should create dread, conflict, or absurdity
Not just state an impressive fact. The hook's job is to make someone feel something uncomfortable enough to stay.

- ❌ "My AI was wrong eighty-four percent of the time and there was one specific change I made that dropped it to eight." (impressive stat, but nobody cares about an app they've never heard of)
- ✅ "I built an app that was wrong about almost everything. And people were using it." (confession energy, dread, makes you need to know what happened)

### 4. Use specifics over vague feelings
Actions and concrete details always beat emotional labels.

- ❌ "It was my least favorite idea" (opinion, vague)
- ✅ "I almost threw it away" / "I almost didn't put it on the list" (action, specific, creates tension)

- ❌ "That changed everything for me" (generic inspirational)
- ✅ "It was ugly. But it worked. And that was enough to keep going." (honest, specific, earned)

### 5. Lean into loss of control over hard work
"I worked really hard for a long time" is never interesting. What's interesting is when something took over and you lost track of time/reality.

- ❌ "I sat down at 9 PM to fix one thing on my app and I didn't get up until all twenty-one features were built." (humble brag about working hard)
- ✅ "I blacked out vibe coding for twelve hours and woke up with twenty-one features I didn't plan to build." (loss of control, something happened TO you)

### 6. Use stronger verbs
Weak verbs soften impact. Strong verbs create movement.

| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| asked | told |
| doesn't work | broken |
| bare bones | science project |
| was wrong | was lying to people |
| looked at why | dug into every single flag |
| got eliminated | killed |
| the winner was | the only one left? |

### 7. CTAs should match the video's energy
If the video was intense, the CTA should carry that energy. If the video was vulnerable, the CTA should match.

- ❌ "Follow if you're curious what happens next" (passive, low stakes)
- ✅ "Follow if you want to watch this thing blow up or crash" (uncertain outcome, real stakes)
- ✅ "Follow to see if it actually works" (honest, vulnerable)

### 8. Self-deprecating humor lands
Your audience respects honesty more than polish. When you can make fun of yourself or your situation, do it.

- "It looked like a science project" (about your own app)
- "Like a toxic ex I couldn't commit" (about your own indecision)
- "I let a very expensive hairless machine decide" (about AI)
- "It doesn't have a legal team. It has me." (about your solo operation)

## Anti-Patterns to Watch For

These are the specific failure modes that show up most often in pipeline-generated scripts:

1. **The double-say:** Stating something, then restating it in different words in the next sentence. Always delete one.
2. **The buffer sentence:** A sentence that exists only to set up the next sentence. ("And the wild part is..." / "There was one specific change..." / "The results were not great.") Delete it and let the payload sentence land on its own.
3. **The explainer after the analogy:** Making a comparison, then explaining the comparison. ("It was like a video game at 8 PM. That exact feeling but with code." — the viewer already got it.)
4. **The corporate closer:** Ending with language that sounds like a startup blog instead of a person. ("Turned the whole thing from a prototype into an actual product" → "By morning, RentShield was a real app.")
5. **The grocery list:** Listing things for comprehensiveness instead of rhythm. If the list doesn't build toward something, cut it or pick the best one.
6. **The passive payoff:** Using vague emotional language where a concrete detail would hit harder. ("Changed everything for me" → what specifically changed? Say that instead.)
7. **Telling a story instead of storytelling:** Recounting events in chronological order as a voiceover monologue — "I did this, then I did this, then this happened." This is telling a story. Storytelling means: one small moment, show what happened through visuals + voiceover + B-roll, make the viewer FEEL the emotion instead of describing it. If the script reads like a timeline recap with no visual direction, no scene changes, and no moments where the screen does the talking — it's telling, not storytelling. Rewrite it.

## Production Layer — Storytelling, Not Telling a Story

Scripts are not voiceover monologues. If a script is just words Jay says to camera with no visual direction, it's "telling a story" — not storytelling. Every script must include the production layer that makes it a VIDEO, not a podcast clip.

### The Rule
No more than 3 consecutive voiceover lines without a production cue. If you've written 3 lines of dialogue in a row with no 📷, no text overlay note, no scene change — stop and add one.

### What to include
- **📷 Shot/B-roll direction:** What the viewer SEES while Jay is talking. Screen recordings, app demos, whiteboard shots, lifestyle B-roll, reaction shots. Be specific — "📷 screen recording of the lease upload flow" not just "📷 phone screen."
- **Text overlays:** Words on screen that add a layer — not just transcribing the voiceover. Numbers, labels, emphasis words, counters. These reinforce the point visually.
- **Scene changes:** Cut to a different angle, location, or visual at least every 10-15 seconds of script time. If the viewer sees the same frame for more than 15 seconds, attention drops.
- **Moments where the visual carries the story:** Sometimes the best move is to STOP talking and let the screen do the work. A screen recording of results loading, a before/after screenshot, a number on screen — these moments don't need voiceover.

### The Test
Read the script and imagine filming it. If you could film the entire thing as a single talking-head shot with no cuts and nothing would be lost, the production layer is missing. Rewrite until the visuals are doing at least 30% of the storytelling.

### Anti-pattern: The Voiceover Monologue
❌ Script that's just paragraphs of Jay talking with a single "📷 phone showing the app" dropped in the middle. That's a podcast with a thumbnail, not a video.

✅ Script where every 2-3 lines have a visual beat — a screen recording, a cut to B-roll, a text overlay, a number on screen — and at least one moment where the visual tells the story without any words.
