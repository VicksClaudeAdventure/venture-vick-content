## Creator Profile: @yohnmav
**Platform:** TikTok
**Series assignment:** Real Estate
**Format tags:** S (serialized rehab doc), T (teach industry mechanics), D (documentary vlog), E (episodic problem-solving)
**Outliers analyzed:** 8

### Recurring Hook Patterns
- **Pattern 1: Day-counter + mission anchor** — Grounds viewers in the ongoing rehab narrative like a reality show episode. Examples: "Today's day seven of saving my local bowling time." / "67 days ago, I told the internet I would rehab an entire bowling alley..." / "65 days ago, I told the internet that I would save this neighbor..." — appears in 3 of 8.
- **Pattern 2: Timeline retrospective + stakes escalation** — "[X time] ago I [bought/told the internet] Y. Now [bigger stakes]." Examples: "This year I told the internet I would rehab an entire bowling alley..." / "Four years ago, I bought a bowling alley that shut down in Chicago. Now, Dalton Bolt also shutting down its lanes for good." — appears in 2 of 8.
- **Pattern 3: Counter-intuitive question hook** — "Is it possible to rehab an entire bowling alley only using Facebook Marketplace?" / "Have you ever wondered when it looks like behind a bowling alley? Good, because today I'm gonna show you." — appears in 2 of 8.
- **Pattern 4: Asked-question industry teach** — "People always ask me, what's the hardest part about owning a bowling now?" — educational frame inside the serialized world.

### Script Structure
- **Typical structure:** (1) Anchor hook (day count or retrospective), (2) Current state + what viewers missed, (3) Problem introduction with specifics (walls, machines, pin setters, crack in wall), (4) Stakes (money, time, decision), (5) Vision/theme declaration ("our theme is retro modern"), (6) Open question to audience, (7) "Follow your boy" sign-off.
- **Average length:** 180-300 words.
- **Pacing notes:** B-roll heavy. Scenes move with him through the building. Uses direct asides to the camera ("My house has 28 lanes, which means I have 28 cars to maintain"). Numbers are dropped to establish scale (60 years old, 40,000 sq ft, $6,000/month in parts, half a million followers in 30 days).

### Storytelling/Teaching Mechanics
- **"I told the internet I would..." framing** treats every project as a public promise, making the audience accountability partners.
- **Industry jargon taught casually:** Brunswick A2 pin-setters, string pin machines, USB-C gold rating, masking units. Teaches you the industry while he lives it, so viewers feel like insiders.
- **Underdog-vs-dying-industry arc:** "Bowling is a dying industry" → "everyone called me delusional" → magazine cover + Creativity Award. Frames him as the fool who didn't know it was impossible.
- **Self-aware meta moments:** "Today I'm locking myself in this bathroom until I finish putting down a new floor... it's starting to occur to me how far I've really come." Switches from action to reflection mid-video — earns intimacy.
- **Decision-driven episodes:** "what color should I paint?" "what's the theme?" Open loops get comments and force sequel videos.

### CTA Style
- Signature closer: **"Follow your boy."** / **"Follow your boy!"** — appears in 6 of 8.
- Variants tied to specific narrative stake: "So if you're ready for season two, bye, you boy." / "if you want to see them come to life, probably it won't [follow]." / "If you want to see how it turns out, follow your boy." / "ask away and follow your boy."
- Always forward-looking — you follow to see a specific outcome revealed.

### What Makes Their Outliers Work
- **Serialized worldbuilding:** Town Hall Bowl, Don't Bowl, Dalton Bowl — specific named places with personalities. Feels like a TV show, not clips.
- **"Saving" framing:** Dying industry + one guy refusing to let it die = emotional investment. The bowling alley is the protagonist, he's the hero.
- **Authenticity moments like the bathroom-floor monologue** — unscripted reflection inside the grind is what separates him from every "I bought a business" content creator.
- **Concrete craft details** — tile, pin-setters, murals, paint color. Viewers learn something real and that's why they share.
